💡 FIELD GUIDE QUICK-TALK REFERENCE POINTS:
● The Collapsed Volcano Caldera: Share the ring dike phenomenon. Point out that the
circular chain of mountains flanking the lake is actually the hardened subterranean
magma ring left behind after a massive Cretaceous volcano imploded into itself 130
million years ago.
● The Ice Age Erratics: Highlight the giant boulders scattered through the woods. Explain
how a mile-thick glacier plucked these house-sized granite blocks directly out of the
mountain faces and dropped them here like giant marbles as the ice sheets melted.
● The Loon Ecosystem Indicator: Point out that the Common Loons nesting in the quiet
marsh coves are highly sensitive indicator species. They cannot walk on land and will
completely abandon lakes if human noise or pollution disrupts the shoreline water
quality, making their presence a badge of pure wilderness health.

🌲 The Boreal Taiga Ecology: Moving past the White Mountains shifts you dramatically out of
New England's standard hardwood forests and directly into the rugged Northern Boreal Forest
(Taiga). The climate up here near the Canadian border is harsh, winters are brutal, and the soil
is intensely acidic. The landscape is dominated entirely by dense, fragrant Balsam Fir, Red
Spruce, and stands of white paper birch. The tree branches are heavily draped in Old Man's
Beard—a rare, wispy green lichen that can only grow in environments with absolute, pristine air
quality.
