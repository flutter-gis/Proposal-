🌲 STATION 1 DOSSIER: BEAR BROOK STATE PARK (OAKS CABIN
1)
🌋 Glacial Geology: Bear Brook sits entirely on top of a massive glacial outwash plain formed
12,000 years ago during the retreat of the Laurentide Ice Sheet. Raging torrents of subglacial
meltwater carrying millions of tons of coarse sand and gravel settled here, burying the
underlying bedrock. This left a deep, highly porous sand terrace that completely prevents
standing water from collecting, filtering rainwater straight down into the massive Suncook River
aquifer.
🔥 Rare Ecology & Fire Barrens: Because the sandy soil lacks vital nutrients and drains
instantly, standard New England maples and birches struggle to survive. Instead, this park
preserves a rare, highly sensitive Pitch Pine and Scrub Oak Barrens ecosystem. This unique
habitat relies entirely on natural wildfire cycles to thrive. Pitch pines have thick, armor-like plates
of bark that insulate them from intense heat, and their pine cones are serotinous, meaning they
are sealed shut by a sticky resin that can only be melted open by a forest fire to drop seeds into
the fresh ash.
🛠 The CCC Human History: In the 1800s, early colonial pioneers chopped down the native
pines to establish sheep and cattle hill farms. The poor, sandy soil quickly collapsed under
agricultural pressure, forcing families to abandon their homesteads. During the Great
Depression, the Federal Government acquired this ruined, failed farmland and handed it over to
the Civilian Conservation Corps (CCC) Camp S-51. Hundreds of unemployed young men
lived on-site in military-style camps, hand-hewing logs and hauling local granite to build the
roads, trail structures, and pavilions you see today. Your cabin loop (Bear Hill) was specifically
engineered by the CCC as an isolated camp to give underprivileged inner-city kids an escape
from urban pollution.
🦫 Wildlife & Canopy Targets: Watch the wetlands along Podunk Road for Great Blue
Herons and the Common Beaver. Beavers act as keystone engineers here; their
stick-and-mud dams flood segments of the forest floor, naturally creating rich, slow-moving
marshes that support wood ducks, painted turtles, and rare dragonflies.
